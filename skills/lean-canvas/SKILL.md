# Lean Canvas: The Single-Page Business Model

## What This Is

The Lean Canvas is ONE page that describes how your business actually works. Not how you hope it works. How it *actually* works based on your customer evidence.

Nine cells. No fluff. No visionary thinking without validation. Adversarial challenge on every single cell.

This becomes the reference document for everything that follows — your MVP spec, your pitch, your hiring, your decisions.

---

## The Nine Cells (And the Questions That Will Haunt You)

### 1. Problem

**What are the TOP 3 problems you're solving?**

Not 10. Not 5. Three.

**Adversarial challenge:**
- Did anyone actually mention this problem in your interviews?
- How many people mentioned it?
- Was it their biggest problem or a "nice to have"?
- Can you quote them describing it?

**Red flags:**
- "Users will struggle with X" (no interview evidence)
- "I think people have this problem" (you think, or they said so?)
- A problem only 1 person mentioned (not a market problem, just one person's problem)
- A hypothetical problem (never actually voiced by a customer)

**What I'll do:** Cross-reference your EVIDENCE-LOG.md. For each problem, I'll count: How many interviews mentioned this unprompted? How many mentioned it after you asked?

**Strong answer:** "Based on 7 interviews, customers spend 5+ hours/week manually updating spreadsheets. 6 out of 7 mentioned this as their biggest bottleneck. They gave specific examples of lost revenue/time."

**Weak answer:** "People probably struggle with managing their data. I think there's a big opportunity here."

---

### 2. Solution

**What are the TOP 3 features that solve the top 3 problems?**

Again: three. Not ten.

**Adversarial challenge:**
- Is this a vitamin or a painkiller?
  - Vitamin: Nice to have. "Would be cool if..."
  - Painkiller: Solves immediate, desperate pain.
- Did anyone ask for this solution, or are you inventing it?
- Is this the simplest way to solve the problem, or are you over-engineering?
- Can you build this in 4-6 weeks?

**Red flags:**
- Solution doesn't directly address a problem mentioned in interviews
- Solving the symptom instead of the root cause
- Three features that each solve different problems (you don't have a focused solution)
- Features that sound good but nobody asked for them

**Strong answer:** "Auto-sync from five data sources into one dashboard. Reduces manual data entry from 5 hours to 15 minutes. Customers specifically asked for 'one source of truth' and mentioned they use 5 different tools."

**Weak answer:** "A beautiful dashboard with mobile app, AI recommendations, and team collaboration features."

---

### 3. Key Metrics

**What is THE ONE metric that matters at this stage?**

Not three metrics. One.

The metric you'll obsess over for the next 3 months.

**Adversarial challenge:**
- Is this a vanity metric (looks good, means nothing)?
- Can you actually measure it?
- Does it move when the business gets better?

**Red flags:**
- Sign-ups (useless — anyone can sign up)
- Download count (people download then uninstall)
- Visits (they might not be using it)
- DAU if your product is for weekly/monthly use
- Any metric that doesn't indicate *actual value delivery*

**Strong metrics:**
- Paying customers
- Customers who use feature X weekly
- Repeat purchase rate
- Dollar amount paid
- Net retention rate (paying customers coming back)

**Strong answer:** "Paying customers. Nothing else matters. First milestone: 10 paying customers in 90 days."

**Weak answer:** "Sign-ups, engagement, and retention. We'll measure daily active users, weekly active users, and monthly retention rate."

---

### 4. Unfair Advantage

**What can't be copied? What do you have that your competitors don't?**

**Adversarial challenge:**
- If this isn't true, you have no unfair advantage.
- "We're better" is not an unfair advantage. That's a competitive advantage.
- Network effects with 0 users is not an unfair advantage yet.
- "Our team is smart" is not an unfair advantage.

**Red flags:**
- "First mover advantage" (they can copy you)
- "We're better at X" (so can they)
- "We have great technology" (so can everyone else)
- "Our team is passionate" (every team is passionate)
- "Network effects" (with zero users, this doesn't exist yet)
- "Brand" (you have no brand yet)

**Real unfair advantages:**
- ✅ Exclusive data or access (you have a dataset nobody else has)
- ✅ Existing user base (you can cross-sell to 1M existing customers)
- ✅ Genuine network effects (but ONLY if you already have critical mass)
- ✅ Regulatory moat (licenses, certifications)
- ✅ Proprietary technology that takes years to replicate
- ✅ You are the customer (you built this for yourself and 100k people like you)

**Strong answer:** "I am the target customer. I've spent 10 years in enterprise sales. I know the pain point better than anyone. I can sell to my 500-person network of enterprise buyers."

**Weak answer:** "Network effects. Once we have 1,000 users, it becomes sticky. Also, we'll have great customer service."

---

### 5. Channels

**How do you actually reach your customers?**

Not "social media." Not "word of mouth." Specific.

**Adversarial challenge:**
- Can you reach them this way?
- Have you validated that this channel works?
- Is this the primary way your customers find solutions like this?
- How much does it cost per customer?

**Red flags:**
- "Social media" (which platform? which audience? which content?)
- "Word of mouth" (works AFTER you have product-market fit, not before)
- "Influencers" (you don't have budget or relationships yet)
- A channel you haven't tested
- A channel your target customer doesn't use

**Strong answer:** "Direct outreach to CTOs on LinkedIn in Series B SaaS companies. We've validated this works: 3 out of 10 cold messages resulted in a 30-min call. Customer acquisition cost: $50 in LinkedIn ads per meeting scheduled."

**Weak answer:** "Social media marketing, content marketing, and partnerships."

---

### 6. Customer Segments

**Who are you building for? Be specific.**

Not "businesses." Not "people who have the problem." Specific early adopters.

**Adversarial challenge:**
- Did you actually interview people in this segment?
- Would they describe themselves this way?
- Are they easy to reach?
- Are they big enough to build a business?
- Are they desperate enough to use version 1.0?

**Red flags:**
- "Anyone with X problem" (too broad)
- A segment nobody from your interviews actually matched
- Targeting everyone equally (you need a beachhead)
- A segment that's hard to reach

**Strong answer:** "Freelance content creators (writers, designers, video creators) with $3k-10k/month revenue who use Stripe and need to track project income for taxes. Located in US. Willing to pay $30-50/month."

**Weak answer:** "Freelancers and small businesses."

---

### 7. Cost Structure

**What does it cost to run this business? Be honest.**

Include your time at market rate. Founders don't work for free in the math.

**Adversarial challenge:**
- What's your actual burn rate?
- How long until you break even?
- Are you being realistic about infrastructure costs?
- What costs will explode as you grow?

**Red flags:**
- Not including founder's time
- Assuming free tier forever for services that charge at scale
- Forgetting about customer acquisition cost
- Ignoring infrastructure costs that grow with users

**Strong answer:**
```
Monthly burn: $8k
- Founder salary (market rate): $4k
- AWS/infrastructure: $1.5k
- Tools and services: $1k
- Operating costs: $1.5k

Runway on $25k savings: 3 months
Break-even: 50 paying customers at $30/month = $1.5k MRR (covers ~19% of burn)
```

**Weak answer:** "We'll be lean. Minimal burn. We'll bootstrap."

---

### 8. Revenue Streams

**How do customers actually give you money?**

Not "we'll figure it out later."

**Adversarial challenge:**
- Did customers indicate they'd pay this way?
- Is this a real business model at your scale, or are you guessing?
- Does the math work?

**Red flags:**
- "Free with ads" (you have 0 users, ads don't work)
- "Freemium" (most freemium businesses fail)
- "Marketplace take rate" (you need critical mass first)
- "Enterprise licensing" (you don't have an enterprise yet)
- No answer at all

**Strong answer:** "B2B SaaS. $29-99/month subscription based on feature tier. We've validated willingness to pay: 2 customers during interviews asked 'how much would this cost?' and seemed receptive to $50/month."

**Weak answer:** "We'll launch free and figure out monetization later. Maybe freemium or marketplace fees."

---

### 9. Unique Value Proposition

**One sentence. A customer reads it and thinks, 'Yeah, that's exactly what I need.'**

Not marketing fluff. Not a tagline. A sentence that your best customer would repeat to a friend.

**Adversarial challenge:**
- Is this meaningful to your customer, or is it jargon?
- Is it unique to you or generic?
- Would a customer actually say this about your product?
- Does it point to a real problem they confirmed in interviews?

**Red flags:**
- Jargon only you understand
- Solving a problem nobody mentioned
- Generic ("We make [problem] easier")
- Too long (if it takes more than 10 seconds to say, rewrite it)
- Not validated by your customer evidence

**Strong answer:** "Automatically track project income and export tax-ready reports so freelancers stop stress-spending hours on accounting."

**Weak answer:** "The next-generation platform for [problem] using AI and machine learning to deliver innovative solutions."

## The Canvas Review Process

I will:

1. Read your INTAKE.md, DESIGN-DOC.md, EVIDENCE-LOG.md, MARKET-RESEARCH.md, FOUNDER-PROFILE.md
2. Challenge every single cell against your evidence
3. Point out where you're guessing vs. where you have validation
4. Force rewrites on cells that don't have evidence
5. Produce a final LEAN-CANVAS.md that is bulletproof
