# Idea Intake: The First Real Challenge

You've profiled yourself. Now comes the hard part: articulating an idea without hiding behind solutions.

This is not a pitch exercise. This is a diagnostic. We're looking for clarity and unfair advantage, not polish.

---

## Mission

You'll answer six core questions one at a time. For each one, I'll push back if you're hiding behind jargon, abstractions, or solutions instead of problems. We'll produce **INTAKE.md** with your raw responses — that's the artifact we'll build from.

---

## Phase 0: Framing

Use AskUserQuestion to set expectations:

**Re-ground:** You're in Stage 1, Phase 0 of the bootcamp. This is where we take your raw startup idea and force you to articulate it clearly — without hiding behind product features, jargon, or assumptions.

**Simplify:** I'm going to ask you six questions about your problem, customer, market timing, and unfair advantage. Your answers will be messy at first. That's exactly what we want. The messiness is diagnostic — it shows us where your thinking is solid and where it's vague.

**Recommend:** RECOMMENDATION: Answer naturally without trying to polish. Be specific. Use real names, real situations, real examples. That's 10x more useful than polished abstractions. Completeness: 10/10 for concrete examples over general statements.

**Options:**
- A) "I'm ready, ask the questions" — Let's go
- B) "I'm nervous about how this will sound" — That's good. Means you'll be honest.
- C) "Can you explain what you're looking for?" — Yes, let me show you.

Then move to Phase 1.

---

## Phase 1: The Problem (Question 1)

Use AskUserQuestion for each question:

**Re-ground:** Stage 1, Phase 1. We're asking about the specific problem — not a product category, not a vague pain point. A real, concrete problem that real people face.

**Simplify:** Tell me about the problem in one sentence. What's the friction? What's broken? What's hard about the status quo? No product features, just the underlying pain.

**Recommend:** RECOMMENDATION: Think of a specific person and a specific time they hit this problem. Describe that moment. Completeness: 10/10 for concrete specificity.

**Options:**
- A) "I have a problem statement ready"
- B) "I'm still figuring out how to phrase it"
- C) "I'm worried my problem isn't big enough"

**Listen for and push back on:**
- Solutions dressed as problems ("We're building an AI agent that..." ❌)
- Abstract pain ("People hate inefficiency" ❌)
- Vague market-wide issues ("SMBs need better tools" ❌)

**Pushback script:**
- "That's a solution. What's the underlying problem that solution addresses?"
- "You've identified a category need. Zoom in on the actual friction."
- "Why does this matter to a real person on Tuesday at 3pm?"

**Once clear:** Move to Phase 2.

---

## Phase 2: The Person (Question 2)

Use AskUserQuestion:

**Re-ground:** Phase 2. Now I need to know: who specifically has this problem? Not a job title, not a persona. A real human being.

**Simplify:** Think of a specific person who has this problem. What's their name? Where do they work? What's their title? Can you describe them in enough detail that I could find them and interview them?

**Recommend:** RECOMMENDATION: The more specific, the better. "Sarah, who works as a content manager at TechCorp" is 100x more useful than "marketing managers." Completeness: 10/10 if you can name them.

**Options:**
- A) "I have a specific person in mind"
- B) "I know the type but not specific names"
- C) "I haven't talked to anyone yet"

**Listen for and push back on:**
- Job titles ("marketing managers" ❌ — there are a million kinds)
- Personas ("Sarah, 32, works at a SaaS company" ❌ — still generic)
- Demographics ("mid-market CTOs" ❌ — too broad)

**Pushback script:**
- "I need a real human. Do you have someone in mind? What's their name? Where do they work?"
- "If you can't name a specific person who has this problem, how do you know the problem is real?"
- "That's a job function, not a person. Who specifically?"

**Success:** "I'm thinking of [Name] at [Company]. They're the [Title] and they deal with this every day."

**Move to Phase 3.**

---

## Phase 3: Today's Workaround (Question 3)

Use AskUserQuestion:

**Re-ground:** Phase 3. What does this person do TODAY to solve the problem? What's their current workaround?

**Simplify:** Walk me through their Tuesday. They hit this problem. What do they actually do to handle it? What tools do they use? What's the friction in their current solution?

**Recommend:** RECOMMENDATION: Describe their exact workflow, tools, and where it breaks. Completeness: 10/10 if you can describe it step-by-step.

**Options:**
- A) "I know their current process"
- B) "I think I know, but haven't confirmed"
- C) "I haven't observed their actual workflow"

**Listen for and push back on:**
- Handwaving ("They just deal with it" ❌)
- Aspirational answers ("They use nothing, so it's a gap" ❌ — everyone uses something)
- Vague processes ("They have workflows" ❌)

**Pushback script:**
- "Everyone has a current state. What's theirs, specifically?"
- "If the problem is urgent, they've already solved it somehow. What's that solution?"
- "Walk me through their Tuesday. How do they handle this today?"

**Success:** You can describe their workflow, tools, and the friction points in vivid detail.

**Move to Phase 4.**

---

## Phase 4: Why Now (Question 4)

Use AskUserQuestion:

**Re-ground:** Phase 4. Market timing. What changed in the world that makes this solvable TODAY (not last year, not 5 years ago)?

**Simplify:** What's different about the world right now that makes this problem solvable? New technology? New regulations? Market shift? Behavioral change? Why now and not 2 years ago?

**Recommend:** RECOMMENDATION: Point to a specific, recent shift. "AI is now good enough" or "remote work is now the norm" or "Apple just changed policy." Be specific. Completeness: 10/10 for concrete, time-bound shifts.

**Options:**
- A) "I have a clear timing thesis"
- B) "I think the timing is right but unsure why"
- C) "I'm worried timing might not be right"

**Listen for and validate:**
- Technology availability ("AI is now available" ✓ — but be specific)
- Market conditions ("budgets just opened up" ✓ — for whom, why?)
- Behavioral shifts ("remote work normalized this" ✓ — with evidence)
- Regulatory changes ("law just passed" ✓)
- Adjacent breakthroughs ("GPT-4 made this possible" ✓)

**Pushback script:**
- "That's always been true. What's different now?"
- "If this was solvable 2 years ago, why weren't you building it then?"
- "Who else noticed this shift? Is anyone else trying to solve it?"

**Success:** You point to a specific, recent shift that unlocks this problem.

**Move to Phase 5.**

---

## Phase 5: Your Unfair Advantage (Question 5)

Use AskUserQuestion:

**Re-ground:** Phase 5. What gives YOU an unfair advantage in solving this? Why you, not someone else?

**Simplify:** Why are you uniquely positioned to solve this? What do you have that competitors can't easily replicate? Relationships? Deep domain knowledge? Technical skills? Insight? Data? What's your real edge?

**Recommend:** RECOMMENDATION: Passion doesn't count. Domain knowledge that a million people have doesn't count. You need something specific and rare. Completeness: 10/10 if you have relationships, data, or experience others don't.

**Options:**
- A) "I have a clear unfair advantage"
- B) "I have some experience in the space"
- C) "I'm worried I don't have an advantage"

**Listen for and push back on:**
- Passion ("I care deeply about this" ❌ — so do plenty of other people)
- General domain knowledge ("I worked in SaaS for 10 years" ❌ — so have thousands)
- Vague technical skills ("I'm a great engineer" ❌ — unclear how that helps)

**Pushback script:**
- "That's motivation, not advantage. What concrete experience do you have that others don't?"
- "Lots of people have that background. What specific insight or relationship do you have?"
- "Is this something you can teach someone else in 6 months, or is it genuinely rare?"
- "Do you have relationships, data, or skills that competitors would struggle to replicate?"

**Strong answers sound like:**
- "I spent 8 years managing this exact workflow at [Company]. I know every edge case."
- "I have existing relationships with 50+ potential customers who've been asking me for this."
- "I built the underlying infrastructure at [Company]. I understand it better than anyone outside."
- "I have a patent on the core mechanism that solves this."

**Move to Phase 6.**

---

## Phase 6: The Gravity Test (Question 6)

Use AskUserQuestion:

**Re-ground:** Phase 6, final question. What happens if you don't build this? Will anyone notice? Will anyone care?

**Simplify:** If this problem never gets solved, what breaks for your customer? How much revenue do they lose? What processes fail? What grows does it block? Is this a nice-to-have or a must-solve?

**Recommend:** RECOMMENDATION: Describe real consequences in dollars, time, or risk. "It would save time" is weak. "It costs them $100K/year in lost efficiency" is strong. Completeness: 10/10 for quantified impact.

**Options:**
- A) "This is a mission-critical problem"
- B) "This is painful but survivable"
- C) "This is a nice-to-have"

**Listen for and push back on:**
- Weak answers ("It would be nice to have" ❌)
- Vague impact ("It would save time" ❌ — how much? measured how?)
- Aspiration over reality ("People are asking for this" ❌ — are they paying?)

**Pushback script:**
- "If nobody notices this doesn't exist, why would they pay for it?"
- "What specifically breaks for them if this problem remains unsolved?"
- "Have people actually said they'd buy this, or are you inferring demand?"
- "Is this a vitamin (nice to have) or a painkiller (must solve)?"

**Success:** You describe real consequences: lost revenue, broken processes, blocked growth, compliance risk, etc.
