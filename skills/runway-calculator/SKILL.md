# Runway Calculator

This is the skill that makes founders confront reality.

Most founders know roughly how much money they have. Most have no idea how much they're spending. And almost none have honestly modeled what happens when the two collide.

This skill forces the math. It's uncomfortable. But it's the math that determines whether you have 2 months or 18 months to make something work. And that number changes *everything* about what you should be doing.

---

## Why This Matters

Let me be direct: **Hope is not a financial plan.**

If your runway calculation requires:
- Everything going perfectly
- All timelines being accurate
- No unexpected costs
- Immediate customer traction

...then you don't have a plan. You have a hope. And hopes run out of money just like bad plans do.

This skill exists to:
1. Calculate your honest runway in months
2. Show you the decision deadline (the date you're out of money)
3. Identify what revenue milestones would extend your runway
4. Surface whether your burn rate is sustainable
5. Expose if your timeline is realistic or fantasy

---

## Core Calculation: Monthly Burn Rate

Burn rate is simple: **monthly spending - monthly revenue = monthly burn.**

But most founders miscalculate because they forget to include opportunity cost.

### What to Include in Monthly Spend

**Direct costs:**
- Hosting (AWS, Heroku, etc.)
- Tools & subscriptions (Stripe, GitHub, project management, analytics, etc.)
- Marketing & acquisition spend (if you're spending money on ads, events, etc.)
- Salaries (if you're paying anyone, including contractors)
- Insurance, legal, accounting

**Opportunity cost (the part most founders skip):**
- **Founder's time at market rate:** If you're full-time and not paying yourself, what would you earn working elsewhere? This IS a cost to your business — it's your opportunity cost.
- **Example:** You're an engineer who could earn $150k/year at a job. Full-time on startup = $12,500/month opportunity cost, even if you're not paying yourself.
- **Why include it?** Because it's REAL money you're giving up. Your runway isn't "how long until I starve" — it's "how long until I can't afford to keep making this bet."

### Scenario-Based Burn Rate

Calculate three scenarios:

| Scenario | Definition | Use Case |
|----------|-----------|----------|
| **Optimistic** | Timeline goes to plan, customer acquisition is smooth, no major surprises | Best case |
| **Realistic** | Timeline takes 1.5x longer, customer acquisition is harder than expected, 1-2 surprises | What will actually happen |
| **Pessimistic** | Timeline takes 2x longer, customer acquisition tanks, multiple surprises, unexpected costs | Plan for this |

**Example breakdown (monthly):**

**Optimistic scenario:**
- AWS + tools: $2,000
- Marketing: $1,000
- Founder opportunity cost: $10,000
- **Total burn: $13,000/month**

**Realistic scenario:**
- AWS + tools: $3,000 (more testing environments, more tooling)
- Marketing: $3,000 (customer research, some ads)
- Founder opportunity cost: $10,000
- **Total burn: $16,000/month**

**Pessimistic scenario:**
- AWS + tools: $5,000 (scaling issues, extra storage)
- Marketing: $5,000 (harder customer acquisition)
- Contractor help hired: $3,000 (you're stuck, need backup)
- Founder opportunity cost: $10,000
- **Total burn: $23,000/month**

## Runway Calculation

Once you have burn rate, runway is straightforward:

**Runway (months) = (Savings + Current Funding) / Monthly Burn**

### Example

You have $100,000 saved. Monthly burn:
- Optimistic: $13k → Runway = 7.7 months
- Realistic: $16k → Runway = 6.25 months
- Pessimistic: $23k → Runway = 4.3 months

**Translation:** You have between 4 and 8 months, depending on how things go.

### The Decision Deadline

Take your realistic runway and subtract 1 month as a buffer. That's your **decision deadline** — the date by which you need to make a decision (raise funding, find paying customers, pivot, or shut down).

**Example:** If realistic runway is 6 months, your decision deadline is 5 months from today.

Mark this on your calendar. This is not an estimate. This is the date that forces a choice.

## Revenue Milestones

Now the critical question: **What revenue would extend your runway?**

### Calculate Milestone Revenue

For each time horizon, ask: "What monthly revenue would extend my runway by X months?"

**Formula:**
- To extend runway by 1 month: Revenue = Monthly Burn - (extra month of cash / total months of remaining runway)
- Simpler: At what monthly revenue does monthly burn = monthly revenue? (That's "ramen profitable")

### Example (Realistic Scenario: $16k/month burn, $100k savings)

**Current runway: 6.25 months**

- **$5k/month revenue:** Reduces burn to $11k/month → extends runway to 9+ months
- **$8k/month revenue:** Reduces burn to $8k/month → extends runway indefinitely (ramen profitable!)
- **$12k/month revenue:** Reduces burn to $4k/month → stretches runway very far

**The question:** Which milestone is *realistic* by which date?
- Can you get to $5k MRR in month 3? (That's doable for some products)
- Can you get to $8k MRR (ramen profitable) by month 5? (Harder, but possible)
- Can you get to $12k MRR? (Rare at early stage without distribution)

If the answer to all of these is "no," your business model might not work with your burn rate. That's valuable to know.

## Ramen Profitable Threshold

**Ramen profitable** = your monthly revenue covers your monthly burn + basic living costs.

This is the survival line. If you hit this, you can keep operating indefinitely (with tight discipline).

### Calculate It

**Ramen profitable MRR = Monthly Burn Rate**

But let's be honest: if your burn includes $10k founder opportunity cost, you're not truly ramen profitable — you're just not losing cash. You're still sacrificing income.

**Real ramen profitability** = Monthly burn *excluding* full market-rate founder salary, because you're paying yourself below market to survive.

### Example

- **Current burn:** $16k/month
- **Below-market founder salary you'd accept:** $5k/month
- **Ramen profitable threshold:** $16k - $10k (market rate) + $5k (accept rate) = $11k MRR

At $11k MRR, you're covering costs and paying yourself something to survive.

## Burn Rate Sanity Check

Is your burn rate normal for your stage? Ask:

- **Similar-stage startups in your space:** What are they burning?
- **Bootstrapped vs. funded:** Funded startups can afford higher burn. Bootstrapped startups should be lean.
- **Team size:** Solo founder should burn less than founding team of 4.
- **Growth rate:** If you're growing 20% MoM, higher burn might be justified. If you're flat, it's not.

**Red flags:**
- Burn rate increasing without corresponding growth
- High marketing spend with low customer acquisition
- Too much tooling/overhead for your stage
- Paying yourself market rate while bootstrapping

## The Day Job Question

Many founders keep a day job while building. This is a financial and personal decision.

### Model: When to Quit the Day Job

Three factors:

1. **Runway:** If your savings are low, you need either the day job or funding. You can't quit into a 2-month runway.
2. **Confidence:** Do you believe this will work? Quitting on a 50/50 product is foolish. On a 80/20 product, possibly smart.
3. **Signal:** Do you have early traction? Even $100/month in revenue is a signal. Zero revenue is a risk.

### Framework: The Transition

**Phase 1 (Months 0-3, part-time on side):** Build. Get to initial signal (MVP working, some interest, any revenue).

**Phase 2 (Months 3-6, reduce day job):** If signal is good, negotiate flex hours or part-time role. Still have income safety net.

**Phase 3 (Months 6+, full-time):** Only when:
- You have 12+ months of runway (savings or funding)
- OR revenue is covering meaningful burn
- OR you have co-founder taking financial risk

**Never quit into:** A 2-month runway with zero revenue. That's panic, not a business.

## Scenario Modeling in Practice

Let's say you're building an SaaS product:

**Today (Month 0):**
- Savings: $80,000
- Current revenue: $0
- Monthly burn (realistic): $14,000
- Realistic runway: 5.7 months (decision deadline: Month 4.7)

**If Month 3 goes well:**
- You have early customers, $2k MRR (revenue)
- Monthly burn: now $12,000 (less marketing spend needed)
- New runway: ~6.5 months (decision deadline moves to Month 5.5)
- Trend: You bought 1 month

**If Month 3 goes badly:**
- No traction, you increase marketing spend to $5k/month to find signal
- Monthly burn: now $17,000
- Runway: 4.7 months (decision deadline: Month 3.7)
- Trend: You lost 2 months. Decision time is NOW.

This is why the runway calculation is a *living document*, not a one-time thing. You recalculate monthly as circumstances change.

## The Blunt Reality Check

When we finish the calculator, I'm going to give you a one-paragraph "financial reality check." It will be direct, because money is personal but lying about it kills startups.

Examples of what it might say:

- **Optimistic case:** "You have 7 months. That's enough time to build MVP, find initial product-market fit, and decide what's next. You're in decent shape."

- **Realistic case:** "You have 5 months to hit $8k MRR or raise funding. That's tight but doable if customer acquisition works. You need traction, not polish."

- **Pessimistic case:** "If things get harder, you're out of money in 4 months. This is your decision deadline. Plan for your next decision now."

- **Hard case:** "Your burn rate and runway don't align with your timeline. Building an enterprise product in 4 months without co-founder is optimistic. Reconsider your approach or secure more funding."

This is not meant to discourage. It's meant to clarify. Founders who know their constraints make better decisions than founders in denial.
